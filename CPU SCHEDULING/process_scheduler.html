First Come First Served

FCFS · Non-Preemptive · FIFO Queue

Theory

The simplest scheduling algorithm. Processes are executed strictly in the order they arrive in the ready queue.

  • Processes queue up and are served in arrival order — FIFO
  • Non-preemptive: once started, a process runs until completion
  • Simple to implement using a basic queue data structure
  • Can suffer from the "convoy effect" — short jobs stuck behind long ones

✓ Advantages

  • Simple to understand and implement
  • No starvation — every process eventually runs
  • Fair in FIFO environments
  • Good for batch processing workloads

✗ Disadvantages

  • High average waiting time
  • Not suitable for interactive systems
  • Convoy effect hurts overall throughput
  • No priority or urgency consideration

Shortest Job First

SJF · Preemptive (SRTF) or Non-Preemptive

Theory

Selects the process with the smallest CPU burst time. Provably optimal for minimising average waiting time.

  • Non-preemptive: current process runs to completion before scheduler picks again
  • Preemptive (SRTF): if a new process with shorter remaining time arrives, it preempts
  • Optimal for minimising average waiting time
  • Main challenge: accurately predicting burst time in advance

  • Better throughput than FCFS
  • Short jobs don't wait behind long ones
  • Good for systems with known job lengths

✗ Disadvantages

  • May cause starvation for long processes
  • Burst time prediction is difficult in practice
  • Not easily implementable in interactive systems
  • Overhead tracking remaining time (preemptive)

Longest Job First

LJF · Preemptive (LRTF) or Non-Preemptive

Theory

The inverse of SJF — LJF always selects the process with the largest remaining burst time. It prioritises longer jobs, ensuring heavyweight processes are not starved at the expense of short tasks.

  • Non-preemptive: once chosen, a process runs to completion before the scheduler re-evaluates
  • Preemptive (LRTF): if a newly arrived process has a longer burst time than the current one, it preempts
  • Results in higher average waiting time compared to SJF or FCFS in most scenarios
  • Useful when long jobs carry higher priority (e.g. bulk data processing pipelines)
  • Short processes may experience starvation if long jobs keep arriving

Priority Scheduling

Priority · Preemptive or Non-Preemptive

Theory

Each process is assigned a priority value. The CPU is allocated to the highest-priority ready process.

  • Lower numerical value = higher priority (by convention)
  • Can be preemptive or non-preemptive
  • Priority can be derived from memory requirements, deadlines, or user-defined rules
  • Aging can be used to gradually raise the priority of waiting processes, preventing starvation

  • Flexible — priority can encode business rules
  • Important processes run first
  • Good fit for real-time systems with deadlines
  • Composable with other algorithms

✗ Disadvantages

  • Low-priority processes can starve indefinitely
  • Priority inversion problem in shared resources
  • Requires aging mechanism to be practical
  • Priority assignment can be subjective

Round Robin

RR · Always Preemptive · Time Quantum

Theory

Designed for time-sharing systems — each process gets the CPU for a fixed time slice called the time quantum.

  • Each process gets CPU for at most q time units per turn
  • Always preemptive — processes exceeding their quantum are preempted
  • Ready queue is treated as a circular buffer
  • Too small a quantum → excessive context switching; too large → degenerates to FCFS
  • No starvation — every process gets a turn

✓ Advantages

  • Fair CPU allocation across all processes
  • No starvation by design
  • Good response time for short processes
  • Ideal for time-sharing and interactive systems

✗ Disadvantages

  • Higher average waiting time than SJF
  • Performance heavily depends on quantum selection
  • Context switching overhead with small quantum
  • Ignores process priority or urgency
